## Dossier Entry: On the Matter of Alaventa Alavaxi

![](/Daggerdale/images/chapter7/image64.jpeg)**Subject:** *An analytical review of the
available intelligence concerning Alaventa Alavaxi, a figure peripheral
yet potentially pivotal to the Thistle Ridge affair.*

**Analysis:** *While I have spent most of my thought on the more overt
players in this drama -- the spectral girls, the hag, the goblins -- the
curious case of Alaventa Alavaxi presents anomalies that demand rigorous
examination. Her unique status, when properly considered, offers
significant insight into the underlying currents of Shadow and deceit
that permeated Thistle Ridge.*

**1. Provenance (The Alavaxi Factor):**

**Family:** Alavaxi. A name now synonymous with mystery and, I suspect,
carefully constructed falsehoods.

**Arrival:** Appeared circa 1416 DR, claiming Chessentan origins. Their
arrival, however, reeks of the unusual -- whispers of emerging from
caves near the Iron Hole, talk of realms *beyond* the Underdark. My own
analysis suggests Shadowfell transit is a far more likely explanation,
fitting the later patterns.

**Ancestral Afflictions:** The convenient disappearance of elders
afflicted with a \'skin condition\' after being sent to Castle Radiance?
Highly suspicious. Likely early evidence of Shadowfell taint or the
consequences of whatever pact brought them here. *(Note to Self:
Cross-reference Castle Radiance records, though I suspect Invictus has
already obscured them).*

**Parentage:** Unspecified. A convenient lack of detail, perhaps?

**2. Childhood Associations:**

**Friendship:** Known companion to the three girls whose spirits now
plague the ruins -- Lilly Clarandal, Lilac Alavandor, Poppy Sweetberry.
They frequented the Clarandal estate (Clarandal Farm). Significant
company to keep, placing her squarely within the circle targeted by
later events.

**Appearance:** Pale-skinned. Represented by a doll with similar
features, notably carrying a silver kukri -- an unusual detail for a
child\'s toy.

**3. The Observation of Tergus Clarandal (Winter Solstice, 1439 DR):**

**Context:** Crucially, this occurs *after* the sacrifice of Andarl,
Abrecia, and Dorian earlier that year -- an event signifying the active
presence of manipulative dark forces (\"The Ancient Mother,\" likely
Palus).

**Tergus\' Naiveté:** The soldier Tergus, bless his observant but
ultimately unanalytical mind, noted a change in Alaventa -- subdued,
joyless, dimmed by a \"faint shadow.\" He worried about \"some Shadow
inside her,\" linking it, rather simplistically, only to her family\'s
origins.

**My Deduction:** While Tergus sensed something amiss, he failed to
grasp the likely trigger. The change he saw was almost certainly a
manifestation of the encroaching darkness following the 1439 sacrifice,
resonating within someone potentially predisposed by her Shadow-linked
heritage. A canary in the coal mine, as it were.

**4. Role in the Tragedy (1441 DR):**

**Calix\'s Account:** The fey Contessa spoke of Palus tricking a girl at
\"that farm\" (clearly Clarandal Farm) leading to the sacrifice of \"the
girl from Thistle Ridge and her three friends.\"

**The Ambiguity:** While confirming four young female victims, Calix\'s
phrasing is frustratingly imprecise. Was Alaventa \"the girl from
Thistle Ridge\"? Or was she one of the \"three friends\"? Or was the
fourth victim another girl entirely? While Alaventa was *present* in the
social circle, her specific role as one of the four sacrificed remains
an interpretation based on this vague testimony. *(Note to Self: Fey
accounts require constant critical assessment for ambiguity and
potential misdirection).*

**5. The Anomaly: Soul at Rest:**

**Nemia\'s Assessment:** The ghost hunter Nemia -- whose skills, while
perhaps lacking my finesse, are useful in her specific niche -- declared
Alaventa\'s soul uniquely \"at rest,\" unlike her three companions whose
spirits empower Palus. This is the central mystery surrounding her. Why
the different fate?

**6. Significance & My Hypothesis (The Shadow Vector):**

**Nemia\'s Intuition:** Nemia vaguely described Alaventa as a \"tool, or
path.\" While lacking analytical rigor, this aligns with my own
conclusions.

**My Deductions:** Connecting the dots -- Tergus\' observation of the
\"Shadow\" post-1439 sacrifice, the family\'s suspect origins
(Shadowfell travel/taint), and her unique \'soul at rest\' status --
leads to the most logical hypothesis: Alaventa served as an
unwitting **vector for Shadow influence**. Her nature, perhaps
inherently touched by the Shadowfell due to her ancestry, made her a
conduit. This role as a \"path\" might explain why her soul\'s journey
concluded differently; perhaps fulfilling this function, however
passively, satisfied some condition of the sacrifice or her inherent
nature, allowing release where the others remained trapped as fuel for
Palus.

**7. Summary:**

Alaventa Alavaxi, scion of a suspect family likely steeped in Shadowfell
connections, stands apart. A friend to the sacrificed girls, potentially
a victim herself (though ambiguity remains), her soul alone found peace.
Tergus Clarandal observed a darkness upon her following the 1439
sacrifice. My own analysis posits she was likely a passive vector for
the Shadow, her unique heritage and fate making her a crucial piece -- a
\"tool or path\" as Nemia put it -- it wouldn\'t hurt her to apply
herself to mastering the Common Tongue -- in understanding the full
mechanics of the conspiracy and the Tragedy, even if she does not linger
as a troublesome spirit. Her story is a testament to the subtle, often
unseen ways the Shadow manifested in Thistle Ridge.

## Dossier Entry: Ambassador Arthur Whitmore - The Sidelined Heir & Potential Asset

**Subject:** An assessment of Arthur Whitmore, currently serving as
Ambassador from Charity to Daggerdale, focusing on his background,
political context, and potential utility to Company Chrysalis.

**Background:** Arthur Whitmore hails from Charity, a town whose
peculiar political structure revolves around four founding families
wielding hereditary power as \'Earls\'. Whitmore himself boasts lineage
from two of these houses: **Whitmore** (presumably his father\'s line)
and **Muloch** (his mother, Chelsea, was the eldest sister of the late
Earl Basil Muloch). As of 1481 DR, he was noted to be 30 years of age.
By all accounts, including Sgt. Paros\'s surprisingly adequate
assessment, Arthur presented as a \"stand-up fellow\" and was
trustworthy enough during the investigation into his uncle Basil\'s
murder. Notably, he was known to be favoured by the late Earl.

**The Political Snub:** Herein lies the crux of the matter. Upon Earl
Basil\'s death, Arthur, as the son of the eldest sister, had a strong,
perhaps the strongest, claim to the Muloch title and estate. However,
the viper\'s nest of Charity politics, likely orchestrated by the
ever-ambitious **Chancellor John Ferguson** (head of the Ferguson
house), saw the inheritance diverted. It passed instead to Arthur\'s
younger cousin, **Godwin Ferguson** (son of Basil\'s *middle* sister,
Morgan, who is conveniently married to a Ferguson). A blatant power
grab, consolidating two of the four Earldoms under Ferguson control and
leaving Arthur, the favoured nephew and claimant through the senior
line, out in the cold.

**The Ambassadorship - A Gilded Cage:** Subsequently, Arthur finds
himself appointed Ambassador to Daggerdale. One need not possess my
level of deductive genius to see this for what it is: a classic
political maneuver. He\'s been given a title, a position that sounds
important to outsiders, but it effectively removes him from Charity\'s
internal power struggles. A sinecure. A polite exile. He represents a
town now firmly dominated by the very family that usurped his
inheritance. How much real influence does he wield? Likely very little.
How much resentment does he harbor? Ah, now *that* is the interesting
question. *(Note to Self: Assess Whitmore\'s temperament. Is he
simmering with indignation, or has he resigned himself to his gilded
cage?)*

**Enter Godwin Ferguson & Thistle Ridge:** The plot thickens, as they
say. This same Godwin Ferguson, the cousin who benefited from Arthur\'s
political sidelining, has now bought his way into Company Chrysalis.
Not, I suspect, out of any genuine desire to aid our noble cause, but
purely for the attached benefits: trading rights and, more importantly,
property rights in Thistle Ridge once reclaimed. He seeks to profit from
the very area we bleed for, leveraging the influence his family secured
at Arthur\'s expense.

**Analysis & Potential Leverage:** Arthur Whitmore, therefore, is a man
likely nursing a significant grievance. He was favoured, had a
legitimate claim, and was outmaneuvered by his younger cousin through
the political machinations of the Ferguson patriarch. Now, he sits in
Daggerdale, an Ambassador in name but likely lacking true power from
home, watching his cousin angle to profit from *our* venture.

This creates a potential opportunity for exploitation, should the need
arise.

**Appeal to Resentment:** A man slighted is often a man motivated.
Godwin\'s move into Thistle Ridge provides a direct target for Arthur\'s
potential ire. The idea that Godwin seeks to gain from Thistle Ridge, a
place Arthur now has some connection to via his ambassadorship (however
nominal), could be galling.

**Undermining Godwin:** We could potentially use Arthur as a source of
information (willingly or unwillingly) on Ferguson family dealings or
Charity politics that might hinder Godwin\'s Thistle Ridge ambitions.
Does Arthur know of any vulnerabilities, trade secrets, or political
skeletons?

**Offering Alternative Influence:** While his Charity power base is
neutered, perhaps association with a successful Chrysalis Company offers
Arthur a different kind of influence or satisfaction -- a way to
indirectly strike back or build something outside the Ferguson shadow.

**Shared Enemy (of sorts):** Godwin\'s crass opportunism makes him
potentially distasteful to us as well. While not a direct enemy like
Palus or Netheril, his attempt to profit from our Reclamation via
political maneuvering makes undermining him a potentially satisfying
side objective. Arthur could be the lever.

**Conclusion:** *Ambassador Arthur Whitmore is more than just Charity\'s
representative; he is a potentially disgruntled political exile,
sidelined by the very family whose scion now seeks to capitalize on our
efforts in Thistle Ridge. While his actual power may be minimal, his
likely resentment and unique position make him a person of interest. He
could be a valuable source of intelligence or even a passive ally if his
desire to see Godwin Ferguson inconvenienced outweighs his loyalty to
Charity\'s current leadership. His trustworthiness in 1481 does not
guarantee his current motivations. A situation worth monitoring
closely.*

## Profile: Aisling Maeliticus (circa 1390 - 1439 DR)

This profile synthesizes known facts and reasoned speculation regarding
Aisling Maeliticus, a key figure in the early history of the Sembian
settlement at Thistle Ridge.

1\. Origins and Arrival (Pre-1408 - 1408 DR):

- Birth: Recorded as born in 1390 DR.

- Family Name: Maeliticus. This name is noted as potentially
  significant, with Netherese roots possibly meaning \"Evil\" or
  \"Malevolent,\" while its Thorass (Old Common) roots could relate to
  \"Milestones\" or \"Legacy.\" The family itself is obscure, with no
  available records. *(Further confirmation indicates the surname
  Maeliticus is specifically native to Daerlun, according to Brother
  Vesuvian\'s research).*

- Marriage: Aisling was the third wife of Alvund Alavandor (the man who
  settled Thistle Ridge under that name, potentially an impostor).
  Alvund\'s previous two wives, Senthia and Abrecia Maeliticus, bore the
  same surname as Aisling, suggesting they were her sisters or close
  relatives. Neither of these prior unions produced known offspring.

- Arrival in Thistle Ridge: Aisling arrived circa 1408 DR with her
  husband Alvund and the Clarandal family as part of the Sembian group
  settling the area. They likely came from Daerlun, under circumstances
  suggesting exile or a hidden agenda orchestrated by Netheril
  (potentially involving an entity known as the \"Old Patriarch\" who
  dealt with *other* Alavandors). It is strongly suspected the settlers
  were afflicted by a sterility curse imposed and/or exploited by
  Netheril/Shadovar.

2\. Life in Thistle Ridge (1408 - 1439 DR):

- **The Phylactery**: Soon after arrival, Aisling established an
  apothecary and possibly a school known as \"The Phylactery.\" This
  establishment gained a significant reputation.

  - Fertility Medicines: The Phylactery became particularly renowned for
    producing highly effective fertility medicines. The Yellow Abbey\'s
    1440 survey noted their success bordered on heresy, though no overt
    occultism was proven by official inquiries.

  - Name Significance: The name \"Phylactery\" itself is noteworthy.
    While rooted in Old Chondathan meaning \"safeguard\" or
    \"protection,\" Brother Vesuvian noted it later acquired darker
    connotations associated with unnatural preservation and holding onto
    power. Aisling\'s choice of this name raises questions about the
    true nature of her practices -- were they purely herbal, or did they
    involve more arcane or potentially darker methods?

- **Motherhood & Fertility Success**: Aisling bore Alvund three children
  in relatively quick succession after arriving in Thistle Ridge: Andarl
  (b. 1408), Senthia (b. 1409), and Abrecia (b. 1410). This, combined
  with Saralla Sakurth bearing Claudius Clarandal\'s children only after
  arrival, strongly suggests Aisling\'s expertise at the Phylactery,
  potentially combined with Thistle Ridge\'s inherent vitality, was
  instrumental in overcoming the settlers\' prior infertility/curse.

- **Potential Interaction with Palus/Jannath (Speculation)**: Given the
  later revelations about Palus operating disguised as Jannath/\"The
  Ancient Mother\" and potentially influencing the settlers via the
  hidden temple, it\'s highly plausible Aisling interacted with this
  entity. Did Palus, under guise, provide Aisling with knowledge,
  ingredients, or methods for her fertility cures? Was Aisling an
  unwitting pawn, using techniques supplied by Palus without
  understanding their true source or implications? The success of her
  Phylactery might stem directly from this hidden, manipulative
  influence.

3\. The Tragic Year (1439 DR):

- **Family Loss**: In 1439 DR, Aisling suffered immense personal
  tragedy. Her daughter Abrecia (now Alavaxi), her son Andarl (Mayor),
  and her grandson Dorian Alavandor were all killed during a \"hunting
  trip.\" This event was later revealed (via séance) to be a sacrifice
  orchestrated through trickery by \"The Ancient Mother\" (likely
  Palus).

- **Suicide**: Later that same year (1439 DR), Aisling Maeliticus died
  by her own hand.

4\. Motivation for Suicide (Hypothesized):

- **Discovery of Truth**: The timing strongly suggests Aisling\'s
  suicide was directly linked to the deaths of her children and
  grandson. It is hypothesized that she discovered the true nature of
  their deaths -- that they were murdered/sacrificed through the
  machinations of \"The Mother of the Ancient Faith.\"

- **Realization of Complicity?**: Furthermore, she may have realized
  that \"The Ancient Mother\" was the same entity (Palus/Jannath) who
  had potentially aided her at the Phylactery. This horrifying
  revelation -- that her \'benefactor\' had murdered her family, and
  that the fertility \'cure\' she provided might have been tainted or
  part of a darker bargain -- would create an unbearable conflict. Had
  her work, her success, been built on a foundation of deceit and evil,
  ultimately leading to her family\'s demise? This profound sense of
  guilt, betrayal, and perhaps contamination of her entire life\'s work
  in Thistle Ridge offers a compelling explanation for her suicide.

- **Connection to Alaventa\'s Change**: It is highly unlikely to be
  coincidental that Tergus Clarandal observed the unsettling change
  (\"faint shadow\") in young Alaventa Alavaxi at the Winter
  Solstice *later in the same year* as both the 1439 sacrifice and
  Aisling\'s subsequent suicide. Both Aisling\'s despair and the change
  in Alaventa likely stem from the same root cause: the profound trauma
  and encroaching dark influence unleashed by the sacrifice orchestrated
  by Palus/\"The Ancient Mother.\" Aisling\'s death reflects the
  consequence of discovering the truth, while Alaventa\'s change may
  reflect the impact of that ambient darkness on a potentially sensitive
  individual.

5\. Summary:

*Aisling Maeliticus was a central figure in the early Thistle Ridge
settlement. Hailing from a family associated with Daerlun (Maeliticus),
she arrived with her husband Alvund under mysterious circumstances
possibly linked to a Netherese-imposed curse. She established the
renowned Phylactery, whose success (especially with fertility) likely
stemmed from a combination of her own skill, Thistle Ridge\'s inherent
vitality, and potentially unwitting collaboration with Palus disguised
as Jannath. The tragic, sacrificial deaths of her children and grandson
in 1439 likely led her to uncover the horrifying truth of Palus\'s
manipulation, revealing the dark bargain underpinning their presence and
success in Thistle Ridge. Unable to reconcile this devastating
knowledge, she took her own life later that year, becoming one of the
key tragic figures whose story foreshadowed the final catastrophe of
1441. The darkness surrounding these events also likely manifested in
the change observed in Alaventa Alavaxi around the same time.*
